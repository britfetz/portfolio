VeriHire

A trust-first recruiting platform built on two-sided verification, radical transparency, and fewer, but better, conversations.

Role
ux research · product design
With
co-researcher
Timeframe
10 weeks
Sector
SaaS
Year
2025 — 2026
view full prototype ↗

the problem

Trust is collapsing on both sides of the table.

Digital recruiting has a dual-direction fraud problem. Recruiters get hit with fake candidates, interview imposters, and AI-assisted screen performances. IT professionals get hit with fake recruiters, phishing outreach, and vague job postings that lead nowhere. There is no neutral environment where both sides can prove who they are and engage safely.

We interviewed 20+ people across both sides of the hiring table — 12 IT recruiters and 9 IT professionals — and benchmarked 4 platforms. Here's the cycle we uncovered:

LinkedIn's verification badge doesn't prevent an interview swap-out, and many recruiters can't even get verified despite uploading passports. The tools that exist verify identity at the platform level — but fraud happens at the process level. That gap is where trust collapses.

what we learned

What both sides told us.

"I went through the interviews, same guy the entire time. He was good on his pre-screen. He was good in his interviews. A different person showed up the first day."

Sam · Sr. SaaS Recruiter · 6 yrs in IT

"If it's just 'let's get on a call,' I'm not bothering. It comes down to personalization and real details about the role."

Zak · Sr. Cloud Support Engineer · 5 yrs in IT

"[The LinkedIn badge] is irrelevant for me. I haven't been able to verify my own profile, even though I've uploaded my passport and ID."

Leslie · General Recruiter · 1 yr in IT

After transcription, initial coding, and affinity mapping, five patterns emerged across every interview:

  • Fraud changes behavior on both sides. Recruiters limit who they'll consider; candidates become suspicious and less responsive. A downward spiral of mistrust.
  • Admin burden is part of the trust problem. Overloaded workflows drive the blanket-messaging culture that candidates perceive as spam.
  • Platform verification ≠ process verification. A badge doesn't stop an interview swap-out. Recruiters have watched a different person show up on day one.
  • Trust is currently DIY. Both sides rely on personal heuristics — photo quality, grammar, eye movement, resume consistency — because platform-level proof is weak.
  • Vagueness is interpreted as risk. Candidates want specifics. Vague outreach or vague postings feel scammy and kill engagement.

"Fraud is creating a downward spiral. Recruiters get conservative, candidates get suspicious, and legitimate people on both sides lose access. The fix isn't more platform badges. It's process-level, two-sided trust."

— synthesis across 11 interviews, jan 2026

Two personas crystallized from the research — one from each side of the table.

Persona 01

Alex

sr. it recruiter · 6+ yrs

Behaviors

Lives in LinkedIn Recruiter. Falls back to Dice. Checks photo quality, connections depth, resume-to-profile consistency. Now defaults to video screens because fraud changed the game.

Pain Points

Interview swap-outs. Keyword-stuffed résumés. Platform badges that don't mean anything. High admin load forces blanket messaging — which candidates read as spam.

Persona 02

Jordan

it professional · active or passive

Behaviors

Judges recruiter legitimacy in seconds: sender domain, grammar, mutual connections, personalization, role specificity. Ignores low-effort platforms entirely.

Pain Points

Spam disguised as outreach. "Let's hop on a call" with no details. Vague job postings. Ghosting after interviews — used to pad a pipeline.

the solution

From platform trust to process trust.

Both sides want the same thing: reliable mutual verification, real role details up front, and visible progress through the loop. We designed three features to make that real.

Outreach Meter

The platform refuses to send vague messages.

Every outreach runs through a real-time Transparency Score. Recruiters see which fields are missing — salary, tech stack, location, hiring manager, role scope. Below threshold, the send button is disabled. The score is visible to the recipient, so candidates know exactly how much the recruiter showed up with.

Two-Sided Verification

Both sides prove who they are — before they're in.

Invite-only. IT professionals are vouched for by peers; work history verified by pinging HR emails of prior employers. Recruiters gated by placement history (1+ yr, 35+ placements). Biometric + ID check on both sides. The verified badge lives on every card, every message, every interview loop.

Progress Bar

Silence is no longer a status.

Every application has a live progress bar: Screened → Submitted → Interviewing → Decision. Recruiters can mark a role "On hold" or "Closed" with one click. The status auto-pushes to every candidate's dashboard. Transparency Score goes up for timely closures, down for dormant pipelines.

what's next

The future loop — from invite to hire.

01
Onboarding
Recruiter invited by an existing verified recruiter (1+ yr, 35+ placements). IT pro invited by a peer — vouched for, not signed up.
02
Profile setup
Work history uploaded. The platform pings HR/verified emails of previous employers. Hard data replaces the résumé-photo-grammar heuristic.
03
Detailed outreach
Transparency Score meter gates the send. Vague messages are blocked. The score is visible in the notification the IT pro receives.
04
Initial call
Scheduled inside the platform. Recruiter focuses on culture fit instead of playing detective. Identity was validated before screening began.
05
Progress visibility
Candidate dashboard updates in real time: Screened → Submitted → Interviewing → Decision. Status is a state, not a silence.
06
Closing
Hire marked in-platform. Candidate profile updated to "Verified Hire at [Company]." The placement counts toward the recruiter's own credibility.
Finding

Neighbors

Making knocking on your neighbors door less awkward and more fun.

the problem

Inches apart, worlds away.

In Barcelona, you can live inches from someone — separated by a wall — and never exchange a word. Neighborhoods are rich with culture and local events, but residents lack a modern, considerate way to connect at the building or block level. WhatsApp groups are chaotic. Community apps feel outdated. The result is silence between people who share the same stairwell.

We surveyed 100+ residents and conducted 50+ semi-structured interviews across Barcelona. The numbers told a clear story:

68%
feel disconnected from the people in their building
62%
rarely communicate with those in closest proximity
100%
reported WhatsApp as chaotic and unstructured for community

This isn't a lack of interest. It's a lack of structure. People want to connect — they just don't have a way to do it that feels safe, low-pressure, and worth the awkwardness.

what we learned

Six patterns from 50+ conversations.

  • Disconnection is not a lack of interest. Residents want to know their neighbors but don't have a natural way to start.
  • WhatsApp is overloaded. Important updates get buried in memes and noise. It's a chat tool, not a coordination tool.
  • Trust precedes participation. People won't engage in a community space unless they feel safe and verified.
  • Adoption must be building-led. Individual sign-ups don't work. The building president or a building admin has to bring people in.
  • "Neighbor" means same building. "Neighborhood" means a 10–15 minute walk. The product needs both layers.
  • Local event discovery is broken. Cultural events exist, but word-of-mouth and Instagram are the only channels. There's no hyperlocal feed.

"Residents don't need another place to chat. They need a trusted, structured layer that makes local coordination and awareness simple — without adding social pressure."

— synthesis across 50+ interviews, march 2026

Two archetypes emerged from the research — and they shaped every design decision that followed.

Archetype 01

The Community Steward

place-invested resident

Behaviors

Knows most neighbors · engages in building decisions · values structure and boundaries.

Needs

Clear building announcements. Defined roles. Verified building-only access.

Archetype 02

The Cautious Connector

low-pressure social participant

Behaviors

Responds more than initiates · prefers 1:1 · avoids being first to post · helps when asked.

Needs

Low-pressure requests. Signals others are responding. Notification digest. Verified access.

the solution

A product built on four pillars — and three key decisions.

Everything we heard pointed in the same direction: residents need small acts of neighborliness, local awareness, circular economy at the building level, and digital boundaries that protect the peace. Here's how we turned those pillars into a product.

Verification

A reward, not a barrier.

We separated access from trust. Users land in the product immediately. The verified badge appears on every post, every request, every responder card — creating quiet social pressure to verify. Unverified users can still view content and tap "I can help"; the requester decides whether to engage.

Posting

Templates kill the blank box.

The Post button opens a drawer with five pre-typed templates: Receive a package, Borrow an item, Offer parking, Ask for help, Post an announcement. When "Receive a package" is a standard option, asking a favor stops feeling like an imposition and starts feeling like what the app is for.

Resolution

Multiple helpers, one clean thread.

Any neighbor taps "I can help" — each response opens a private 1:1 thread. The requester sees everyone who offered, with verification status, and chooses who to coordinate with. When marked resolved, all threads archive. Warmth of community, clarity of a direct conversation.
