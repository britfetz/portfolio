VeriHire

A trust-first recruiting platform built on two-sided verification, radical transparency, and fewer, but better, conversations.

01 / context

Trust is collapsing on both sides of the table.

Digital recruiting has a dual-direction fraud problem. Recruiters get hit with fake candidates, interview imposters, and AI-assisted screen performances. IT professionals get hit with fake recruiters, phishing outreach, and vague job postings that lead nowhere. LinkedIn's verification shield exists — but it doesn't prevent an interview swap-out, and many recruiters can't even get themselves verified despite uploading passports. There is no neutral environment where both sides can prove who they are and engage safely.

04 / personas

Who we're designing for.

Persona 01

Alex

sr. it recruiter · 6+ yrs

Behaviors

Lives in LinkedIn Recruiter. Falls back to Dice. Checks photo quality, connections depth, resume-to-profile consistency. Now defaults to video screens because fraud changed the game.

Pain Points

Interview swap-outs. Keyword-stuffed résumés. Platform badges that don't mean anything. High admin load forces blanket messaging — which candidates read as spam.

What They Want

A lightweight "proof" layer so screening isn't the first time identity is validated. Two-sided verification that's reliable and inclusive.

Persona 02

Jordan

it professional · active or passive

Behaviors

Judges recruiter legitimacy in seconds: sender domain, grammar, mutual connections, personalization, role specificity. Ignores low-effort platforms entirely.

Pain Points

Spam disguised as outreach. "Let's hop on a call" with no details. Vague job postings. Ghosting after interviews — used to pad a pipeline.

What They Want

Fewer, higher-signal messages. Proof the recruiter actually looked at the profile. Status visibility through the process — even "no" beats silence.

05 / insight

"Fraud is creating a downward spiral. Recruiters get conservative, candidates get suspicious, and legitimate people on both sides lose access. The fix isn't more platform badges. It's process-level, two-sided trust."

— synthesis across 11 interviews, jan 2026

06 / cross-interview themes

Five patterns we heard from everyone.

01
Fraud changes behavior in negative ways.
Recruiters limit who they'll consider; candidates become suspicious and less responsive. A downward spiral of mistrust.
02
Admin burden is part of the trust problem.
Overloaded workflows drive the blanket-messaging culture that candidates perceive as spam.
03
Platform verification ≠ process verification.
A badge doesn't stop an interview swap-out. Recruiters have watched a different person show up on day one.
04
Trust is currently DIY and informal.
Both sides rely on personal heuristics, photo quality, grammar, eye movement, résumé consistency, because platform-level proof is weak.
05
Vagueness is interpreted as risk.
Candidates want specifics. Vague outreach or vague postings feel scammy and kill engagement.

07 / strategy

Four moves to get from platform trust to process trust.

01
Early role transparency
Recruiters must include a minimum set of details, salary range, tech stack, location, before the platform will let the outreach send.
02
Mutual identity verification
Both recruiters and IT professionals verify before they can join. Invite-only. Recruiters gated by placement history.
03
Clear status visibility
Candidates see live progress on every application. "Pending" is a state, not a silence. Ghosting ends.
04
Fewer, higher-signal messages
Quality is rewarded and volume is penalized. Recruiters who blanket-message lose platform trust score.

08 / design decisions

Three features that make the strategy real.

Decision 01 · Outreach Meter

The platform refuses to send vague messages.

Tension

"If it's just 'let's get on a call,' I'm not bothering." — Zak, IT Professional. Vague outreach kills response rates and trains candidates to ignore everything.

Decision

Every outreach message runs through a real-time Transparency Score meter. Recruiters see which fields are missing — salary, tech stack, location, hiring manager, role scope. Below threshold, the send button is disabled. The score is visible to the recipient in the notification, so candidates know exactly how much the recruiter showed up with.

Decision 02 · Two-Sided Verification

Both sides prove who they are — before they're in.

Tension

"The LinkedIn badge is irrelevant for me." — Leslie, Recruiter. Platform verification doesn't stop interview swap-outs. Many recruiters can't even get verified themselves.

Decision

Invite-only. IT professionals are vouched for by peers; their work history is verified by pinging HR emails of prior employers. Recruiters are gated by placement history (1+ year, 35+ placements) and invited by existing verified recruiters. Biometric + ID check on both sides. The verified badge lives on every card, every message, every interview loop.

Decision 03 · Progress Bar

Silence is no longer a status.

Tension

"Ghosting is interpreted as disrespect." — Neal, CTO. "Needs status visibility — even 'no' is better than silence." The black hole is the #1 thing candidates hate about recruiting.

Decision

Every application has a live progress bar: Screened → Submitted → Interviewing → Decision. Recruiters can mark a role "On hold" or "Closed" with one click, and the status auto-pushes to every candidate's dashboard. The recruiter's Transparency Score goes up for timely closures and down for dormant pipelines.

09 / journey

The future loop — from invite to hire.

01
Onboarding
Recruiter invited by an existing verified recruiter (1+ yr, 35+ placements). IT pro invited by a peer — vouched for, not signed up.
02
Profile setup
Work history uploaded. The platform pings HR/verified emails of previous employers. Hard data replaces the résumé-photo-grammar heuristic.
03
Detailed outreach
Transparency Score meter gates the send. Vague messages are blocked. The score is visible in the notification the IT pro receives.
04
Initial call
Scheduled inside the platform. Recruiter focuses on culture fit instead of playing detective. Identity was validated before screening began.
05
Progress visibility
Candidate dashboard updates in real time: Screened → Submitted → Interviewing → Decision. Status is a state, not a silence.
06
Closing
Hire marked in-platform. Candidate profile updated to "Verified Hire at [Company]." The placement counts toward the recruiter's own credibility.

10 / outcome

What's validated. What's next.

Finding
Evidence
Fraud reshapes behavior on both sides.
Recruiters limit candidate pools; candidates ignore recruiters by default.
Platform verification fails the moment that matters.
Interview swap-outs happen even on verified profiles. Badges are process theater.
Vagueness is the single biggest trust-killer.
Across every IT pro interview, specificity triggered engagement; "quick call?" triggered delete.
Both sides want the same fix.
Reliable mutual verification, real role details up front, visible progress through the loop.

Next: validate the Transparency Score meter with recruiters in a paid concept test, and prototype the invite-only onboarding flow with a small cohort of IT professionals. The research is done. The question now is whether recruiters will accept a system that penalizes volume — and whether IT pros will trust a new platform before a network exists on it.

Neighbors

01 / context

Inches apart, worlds away.

In Barcelona, you can live inches from someone — separated by a wall — and never exchange a word. Neighborhoods are rich with culture and local events, but residents lack a modern, considerate way to connect at the building or block level without the pressure, noise, or awkwardness of a WhatsApp group or an outdated community app.

02 / research

What we did. What we heard.

100+
survey responses
50+
semi-structured interviews
10+
competitors benchmarked
3
archetypes synthesised

what the survey said

68%
feel disconnected from the people in their building.
62%
rarely communicate with those in closest proximity.
100%
reported whatsapp as chaotic and unstructured for community.

cross-interview themes

  • Disconnection is not a lack of interest.
  • WhatsApp is overloaded and unstructured.
  • Trust precedes participation.
  • Adoption must be building-led, not user-led.
  • "Neighbor" means same building. "Neighborhood" means a 10-15 minute walk.
  • Local event discovery is fragmented and outdated.

03 / insight

"Residents don't need another place to chat. They need a trusted, structured layer that makes local coordination and awareness simple — without adding social pressure."

— synthesis across 50+ interviews, march 2026

04 / archetypes

Who we're designing for.

Archetype 01

The Community Steward

place-invested resident

Behaviors

Knows most neighbors · engages in building decisions · values structure and boundaries.

Needs

Clear building announcements. Defined roles. Verified building-only access.

Archetype 02

The Cautious Connector

low-pressure social participant

Behaviors

Responds more than initiates · prefers 1:1 · avoids being first to post · helps when asked.

Needs

Low-pressure requests. Signals others are responding. Notification digest. Verified access.

05 / product pillars

Four things the product has to do.

01
Small acts of neighborliness
Low-pressure ways for neighbors to help each other and break the ice.
02
Local awareness
Hyperlocal life spotlighted within a 15-minute walking radius.
03
Second-hand wins
Circular economy within the building or neighborhood, borrow before you buy.
04
Digital boundaries
Trusted verification and community guidelines that protect the peace.

06 / design decisions

Three decisions that define the product.

Decision 01 · Verification

Verification is a reward, not a barrier.

Tension

Verification is trust — but friction up front kills adoption.

Decision

We separated access from trust. Users land in the product immediately. The verified badge is the reward, appearing on every post, every request, every responder card, creating quiet social pressure to verify without a single push notification. Unverified users can still view content and tap "I can help"; the requester decides whether to engage.

Decision 02 · Posting

Templates kill the blank box.

Tension

The blank message box is the enemy of participation. Our Cautious Connector consistently hesitated to initiate — not out of fear of rejection, but fear of awkwardness.

Decision

The Post button opens a drawer with five pre-typed templates: Receive a package · Borrow an item · Offer parking · Ask for help · Post an announcement. When "Receive a package" is listed as a standard option, asking your neighbor to hold a box stops feeling like an imposition and starts feeling like what the app is for.

Decision 03 · Resolution

Multiple helpers, one clean resolution.

Tension

In WhatsApp, "Can someone help?" generates 15 replies of "I can!" with no accountability and no resolution. The request dies in noise.

Decision

Any neighbor taps "I can help" — each response opens a private 1:1 thread visible only to the requester. The requester sees a list of everyone who offered, with verification status, and chooses who to coordinate with. When marked resolved, all threads archive. Warmth of community, clarity of a direct conversation.
